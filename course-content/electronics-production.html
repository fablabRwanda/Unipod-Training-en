Overview

Welcome to Electronics Production! This week, you'll learn to fabricate printed circuit boards (PCBs) using tools available in your UNIPOD lab. Whether you're milling boards with a CNC machine, cutting them with a vinyl cutter, or using chemical etching, understanding PCB production is essential for creating custom electronics that solve local problems.

In many African contexts, importing ready-made PCBs can be expensive and slow. By learning to produce boards in your lab, you gain the ability to rapidly prototype and iterate on designs. This week builds directly on Electronics Design (where you learned to design boards) and prepares you for Embedded Programming (where you'll write code for your custom hardware).

You'll learn safe operation of milling machines, proper soldering techniques, and how to test and debug your boards. By the end of the week, you'll have fabricated, assembled, and verified a working PCB—a skill that opens doors to creating custom electronics for agriculture, health, education, and more.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Understand PCB production methods and choose the right approach for your project (milling, vinyl cutting, etching, or outsourcing)
  • Operate milling machines safely to fabricate PCBs with appropriate trace widths and clearances
  • Practice surface-mount soldering techniques for small components (0603, 0805, SOIC packages)
  • Inspect and test boards using multimeters, continuity tests, and visual inspection
  • Debug common fabrication issues (broken traces, shorts, misaligned drills) and fix them
  • Source components locally and document suppliers, costs, and availability in your region

Core Concepts & Production Methods

1. PCB Production Methods

There are several ways to make PCBs in a lab. Each has trade-offs:

  • CNC Milling (Most common in UNIPODs): Uses a small end mill to cut away copper, leaving traces. Pros: Fast, no chemicals, reusable. Cons: Limited resolution (~0.2mm traces), tool wear, requires good machine calibration.
  • Vinyl Cutting: Uses a vinyl cutter to create a mask, then etches with chemicals. Pros: Simple, works for large traces. Cons: Lower resolution, requires chemicals.
  • Chemical Etching: Uses photoresist and etchant (ferric chloride or similar) to remove unwanted copper. Pros: High resolution possible. Cons: Requires chemicals, disposal concerns, more steps.
  • Outsourcing: Send Gerber files to a PCB manufacturer. Pros: Professional quality, multi-layer boards, solder mask. Cons: Cost, shipping time, requires internet/credit card.

For most UNIPOD labs, CNC milling is the primary method because it's fast, safe, and doesn't require chemicals.

2. CNC Milling Workflow

The typical workflow for milling a PCB:

  1. Prepare design files: Export your board layout as PNG (monochrome, high resolution) or Gerber files
  2. Generate toolpaths: Use software (MODS, FlatCAM, or machine-specific tools) to create G-code
  3. Secure the board: Tape or clamp a copper-clad board to the machine bed
  4. Set zero point: Establish X, Y, Z origin (critical for accuracy!)
  5. Mill traces: Use a 1/64" (0.4mm) end mill to cut isolation paths around traces
  6. Drill holes: Use a 1/32" (0.8mm) drill bit for component holes and vias
  7. Cut outline: Optionally cut the board outline with a larger bit
  8. Clean and inspect: Remove dust, check for broken traces or shorts

3. Soldering Surface-Mount Components

Surface-mount (SMD) components are smaller and cheaper than through-hole parts. Common sizes:

  • 0603: 1.6mm x 0.8mm (resistors, capacitors)—good for beginners
  • 0805: 2.0mm x 1.25mm (slightly easier to handle)
  • SOIC: Small Outline Integrated Circuit (microcontrollers, ICs)
  • QFN/QFP: Very small, many pins—advanced soldering

Soldering technique:

  1. Apply flux to the pads (helps solder flow)
  2. Place component with tweezers (align carefully!)
  3. Hold component in place, touch one pad with soldering iron
  4. Apply small amount of solder to create a "tack"
  5. Check alignment, then solder remaining pads
  6. Inspect for bridges (shorts between pads) and cold joints

4. Testing and Debugging

Always test your board before programming:

  • Visual inspection: Look for broken traces, shorts, missing components, reversed polarity
  • Continuity test: Use a multimeter to check that traces connect correctly (no breaks, no shorts to ground)
  • Power test: Apply power carefully, check for correct voltages at key points (VCC, GND)
  • Component test: Verify components are oriented correctly (diodes, capacitors, ICs)

Common issues and fixes:

  • Broken trace: Bridge with wire or re-mill the board
  • Short circuit: Use a knife or re-mill to cut the short
  • Cold solder joint: Reheat with iron, add flux if needed
  • Missing component: Solder it in place
  • Reversed component: Desolder, flip, resolder

Mini-Lab: Fabricate and Assemble a Simple Programmer Board

Let's fabricate a simple USB programmer board (like a FabTinyISP or similar) step-by-step.

Step 1: Prepare Design Files

  1. Export your board layout as PNG (monochrome, 1000 DPI) or use Gerber files
  2. You need two images: traces (copper to keep) and outline (board shape)
  3. Invert colors if needed: traces should be white on black background

Step 2: Generate Toolpaths

  1. Open MODS (or your lab's CAM software)
  2. Load your trace PNG
  3. Set parameters:
    • Tool diameter: 0.4mm (1/64")
    • Cut depth: 0.1mm (just enough to remove copper)
    • Offset number: 4 (creates isolation paths around traces)
    • Speed: Follow your machine's recommendations
  4. Generate G-code and save

Step 3: Mill the Board

  1. Secure copper-clad board to machine bed (double-sided tape works well)
  2. Set X, Y zero (usually front-left corner of board)
  3. Set Z zero (touch tool to board surface, then raise slightly)
  4. Run trace milling job
  5. Inspect: Are all traces isolated? Are there any shorts?
  6. If good, run drill job (1/32" bit for holes)
  7. If needed, run outline cut

Step 4: Clean and Inspect

  1. Remove board from machine
  2. Clean with isopropyl alcohol and soft brush
  3. Inspect under good light: check for broken traces, shorts, burrs
  4. Use multimeter continuity test to verify connections

Step 5: Solder Components

  1. Gather all components (check your BOM)
  2. Start with smallest components (resistors, capacitors)
  3. Apply flux, place component, tack one pad, check alignment, solder remaining pads
  4. Move to larger components (microcontroller, connectors)
  5. Double-check orientation of polarized components (diodes, capacitors, ICs)
  6. Inspect for solder bridges—fix with desoldering braid if needed

Step 6: Test and Program

  1. Visual inspection: All components in place? No obvious shorts?
  2. Continuity test: Check power and ground connections
  3. Power test: Apply power carefully, check voltages
  4. If all good, proceed to programming (next week's topic!)

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Group Assignment: Characterize Your Production Process

As a lab team, create a process characterization document:

  • Test different trace widths (0.1mm, 0.2mm, 0.3mm) and clearances (0.1mm, 0.2mm, 0.3mm)
  • Document which combinations work reliably with your machine
  • Measure actual trace widths vs. designed widths (account for tool wear)
  • Test alignment between top and bottom layers (if doing double-sided boards)
  • Document tool life: How many boards can you mill before the bit dulls?
  • Create a "design rules" document that future students can follow

Individual Assignment

Fabricate and assemble a working PCB:

  • Choose a board design (programmer, sensor board, or your own design from Electronics Design week)
  • Mill the board using your lab's process
  • Solder all components correctly
  • Test the board (continuity, power, functionality)
  • If it doesn't work, debug and fix it—document your troubleshooting process
  • Program the board (if it's a programmer or microcontroller board)

Tips for Success

  • Start simple: Don't try to mill a complex board on your first attempt. Begin with a simple design with large traces.
  • Check your files twice: Before milling, verify that your PNG/Gerber files are correct. Broken traces in the file = broken traces on the board.
  • Zero carefully: Accurate X, Y, Z zeroing is critical. Take your time, double-check, and document your zero point.
  • Inspect as you go: After milling traces, inspect before drilling. After drilling, inspect before soldering.
  • Use flux: Flux makes soldering much easier, especially for SMD components. Don't skip it!
  • Test incrementally: After soldering power components, test power before soldering sensitive ICs.
  • Keep spare components: Components are small and easy to lose. Have extras on hand.
  • Document problems: If something goes wrong, document it. Your mistakes help others learn.
