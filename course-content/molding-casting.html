Overview

Welcome to Molding and Casting! This week, you'll learn to create molds and cast parts—a powerful technique for producing multiple copies of objects. Whether you're making soap, resin keychains, or concrete planters, molding and casting enable small-scale production that's perfect for local entrepreneurship.

In African contexts, molding and casting open opportunities for local manufacturing: creating soap for hygiene, concrete products for construction, resin items for crafts, and more. We'll explore different mold materials (silicone, 3D-printed molds, machined molds) and casting materials (resin, concrete, soap, etc.) that are accessible in African markets.

This week builds on Computer-Controlled Machining and 3D Printing (where you learned to create positive forms) and prepares you for Final Project (where you might need to produce multiple parts). By the end of the week, you'll understand how to design molds, mix and pour casting materials safely, and produce repeatable parts.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Design positive and negative forms for molding using CAD software
  • Understand mold materials including silicone, urethane, plaster, and their properties
  • Understand casting materials including resin, concrete, soap, and their mixing ratios
  • Create molds using CNC machining, 3D printing, or hand techniques
  • Mix and pour casting materials safely with proper safety equipment
  • Produce repeatable cast parts from your mold with consistent quality
  • Document material choices considering cost, availability, and safety in African contexts

Core Concepts & Theory

1. Understanding Molds: Positive and Negative Forms

A mold is a negative impression of the object you want to create. The process involves:

  • Positive (Master/Pattern): The original object you want to replicate. Can be 3D printed, CNC machined, or hand-carved. This is what you'll make a mold of.
  • Negative (Mold): The cavity that will hold the casting material. Made from flexible (silicone) or rigid (machined, 3D printed) materials.
  • Cast Part: The final object created by pouring material into the mold.

Design considerations:

  • Draft angles: Slight tapers (1-3°) help parts release from molds
  • Undercuts: Avoid or use flexible molds (silicone) for complex shapes
  • Venting: Small channels allow air to escape during pouring
  • Parting lines: Where mold halves meet—design to minimize visible seams

2. Mold Materials

Different materials suit different applications:

  • Silicone Rubber:
    • Flexible, captures fine details, reusable many times
    • Two-part systems (base + catalyst), cure time 4-24 hours
    • Cost: Moderate (check local availability)
    • Use: Complex shapes, undercuts, fine detail
    • Safety: Wear gloves, work in well-ventilated area
  • Urethane Rubber:
    • Similar to silicone but different properties
    • Can be harder or softer depending on formulation
    • Use: When silicone isn't available or suitable
  • Plaster:
    • Rigid, inexpensive, widely available
    • One-part system, sets in 30-60 minutes
    • Use: Simple shapes, one-time casts, large objects
    • Limitation: Brittle, can't handle undercuts
  • 3D-Printed Molds:
    • Rigid, quick to make, good for prototyping
    • Use: Simple shapes, testing designs before making silicone molds
    • Limitation: Layer lines transfer to cast parts, limited reusability
  • Machined Molds:
    • CNC-milled from wax, wood, or aluminum
    • Smooth surfaces, precise dimensions
    • Use: Production molds, high-precision parts

3. Casting Materials

Choose materials based on your application:

  • Epoxy Resin:
    • Clear or colored, hard, glossy finish
    • Two-part system (resin + hardener), cure time 24-48 hours
    • Use: Keychains, jewelry, decorative items
    • Safety: Wear gloves, eye protection, work in ventilated area
  • Polyurethane Resin:
    • Similar to epoxy, various hardnesses available
    • Faster cure time than epoxy
    • Use: Functional parts, prototypes
  • Concrete:
    • Inexpensive, strong, widely available
    • Mix: Cement + sand + water (typical ratio 1:2:0.5)
    • Cure time: 24-48 hours for handling, 28 days for full strength
    • Use: Planters, construction elements, heavy-duty items
  • Soap:
    • Melt-and-pour or cold-process soap
    • Use: Hygiene products, local entrepreneurship
    • Safety: Handle hot soap carefully
  • Wax:
    • For lost-wax casting (advanced technique)
    • Use: Metal casting processes

4. Safety Considerations

Always prioritize safety when working with casting materials:

  • Ventilation: Work in well-ventilated area or use fume hood
  • Personal Protective Equipment (PPE):
    • Nitrile gloves (not latex—some materials degrade latex)
    • Safety glasses or goggles
    • Apron or old clothes
    • Respirator if working with strong fumes
  • Mixing: Follow manufacturer's instructions exactly. Wrong ratios = failed casts
  • Temperature: Some materials are exothermic (generate heat). Monitor temperature
  • Cleanup: Clean tools immediately after use. Cured materials are hard to remove
  • Storage: Store materials in cool, dry place, away from direct sunlight

Mini-Lab: Create a Simple Silicone Mold and Cast Resin Parts

Let's create a simple two-part silicone mold and cast resin keychains—a perfect project for local entrepreneurship.

Step 1: Design and Create the Master (Positive)

  1. Design in CAD: Create a simple shape (e.g., logo, geometric pattern, text) that's 5-10mm thick
  2. 3D Print or Machine: Create the master using 3D printer or CNC machine
  3. Finish: Sand smooth if needed. The better the finish, the better the cast parts

Step 2: Build a Mold Box

  1. Create a box around your master using:
    • Laser-cut acrylic or cardboard
    • Hot glue or tape to seal edges
    • Leave 10-15mm clearance around master on all sides
  2. Secure master to bottom of box (double-sided tape or hot glue)
  3. Apply mold release (if needed) to master and box walls

Step 3: Mix and Pour Silicone

  1. Measure: Calculate volume needed (length × width × height of box minus master volume)
  2. Mix:
    • Wear gloves and safety glasses
    • Mix base and catalyst according to manufacturer's ratio (typically 1:1 or 10:1)
    • Mix thoroughly for 2-3 minutes, scraping sides of container
  3. Pour:
    • Pour slowly from one corner to avoid trapping air bubbles
    • Pour to cover master by at least 5mm
    • Tap box gently to release air bubbles
  4. Cure: Let cure according to manufacturer's instructions (typically 4-24 hours)

Step 4: Demold and Prepare for Casting

  1. Remove mold from box
  2. Carefully remove master from silicone
  3. Inspect mold for defects (air bubbles, tears)
  4. Clean mold if needed

Step 5: Cast Resin Parts

  1. Prepare: Place mold on level surface, apply mold release if needed
  2. Mix Resin:
    • Measure resin and hardener according to instructions (typically 1:1 or 2:1)
    • Mix slowly for 2-3 minutes to avoid bubbles
    • Add colorant or glitter if desired
  3. Pour: Pour slowly into mold, fill to top
  4. Cure: Let cure 24-48 hours (check instructions)
  5. Demold: Carefully remove cast part from mold
  6. Finish: Sand edges, drill holes for keychain if needed

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Individual Assignment

Design and create a mold, then cast at least two parts from it:

  • Design a positive form (master) in CAD—can be 3D printed or CNC machined
  • Create a mold using one of these methods:
    • Silicone mold (recommended for flexibility and detail)
    • 3D-printed mold (for quick prototyping)
    • CNC-machined mold (for precision)
  • Cast at least two parts using your chosen casting material (resin, concrete, soap, etc.)
  • Document material choices: Why did you choose these materials? What was the cost? Are they available locally?

Group Assignment (Optional but Recommended)

As a UNIPOD team, test different casting materials:

  • Test at least 3 different casting materials (e.g., epoxy resin, polyurethane resin, concrete)
  • Document for each material:
    • Strength (can you break it? how much force?)
    • Surface finish (smooth, rough, glossy, matte)
    • Cure time (how long until you can demold? full cure?)
    • Cost per part (material cost ÷ number of parts)
    • Availability in your region
  • Create a comparison table to help future students choose materials

Tips for Success

  • Start simple: Begin with a simple, flat design before attempting complex 3D shapes.
  • Measure accurately: Use digital scale for mixing ratios. Wrong ratios = failed casts.
  • Mix thoroughly: Scrape sides and bottom of container. Incomplete mixing = soft spots or failed cures.
  • Avoid bubbles: Pour slowly, tap molds to release bubbles, consider vacuum degassing for critical parts.
  • Test materials: Make small test casts before committing to large molds.
  • Document everything: Record mixing ratios, cure times, temperatures—you'll need this for future casts.
  • Clean immediately: Clean tools and containers while materials are still liquid. Cured materials are hard to remove.
  • Consider local materials: Explore locally available alternatives (local resins, concrete mixes) to reduce costs.
