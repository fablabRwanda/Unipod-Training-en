Design Thinking for African Makers

Empowering Africa's Future with the UNIPOD Digital Fabrication Program.
In partnership with Timbuktoo, UNDP, FabLab Rwanda and UNIPODS, we're delivering cutting-edge technology education across Africa.

Overview

Welcome to Design Thinking for African Makers! This week introduces design thinking—a human-centered approach to innovation that starts with understanding people's needs and ends with solutions that are desirable, feasible, and viable.

In African contexts, where innovation must address real community challenges, design thinking provides a structured approach to creating solutions that truly matter. You will learn to frame problems in your community, prototype quickly in the lab, and document your process so others can build on your ideas.

This week sets the foundation for your entire UNIPOD journey. The problems you identify and prototype here will guide your learning throughout the course and may become your final project. By the end of the week, you'll have identified real problems, generated solutions, and created low-fidelity prototypes to test your ideas.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Apply design thinking process including empathize, define, ideate, prototype, and test stages
  • Identify real problems in your community through observation and empathy
  • Map stakeholders and understand their needs, constraints, and contexts
  • Generate multiple solutions through brainstorming and ideation techniques
  • Create low-fidelity prototypes using paper, cardboard, or digital fabrication tools
  • Test prototypes with users and gather feedback
  • Iterate based on feedback and plan improvements for future technical weeks
  • Document the design process so others can understand your thinking and build on your ideas

Core Concepts & Theory

1. The Design Thinking Process

Design thinking is a five-stage process (though stages often overlap and iterate):

  • Empathize:
    • Understand your users and their needs
    • Observe, interview, and immerse yourself in their context
    • Ask: What do they struggle with? What are their goals? What constraints do they face?
    • In African contexts: Consider access, cost, environment, cultural factors
  • Define:
    • Synthesize your research into a clear problem statement
    • Frame the problem from the user's perspective
    • Ask: What is the core problem? Who is affected? Why does it matter?
    • Create a "point of view" statement: "User needs X because Y"
  • Ideate:
    • Generate many possible solutions
    • Brainstorm without judgment—quantity over quality initially
    • Use techniques: mind mapping, sketching, "how might we" questions
    • Ask: How might we solve this problem? What are all possible approaches?
  • Prototype:
    • Build low-fidelity versions of your ideas
    • Use simple materials: paper, cardboard, 3D prints, laser cuts
    • Focus on testing key assumptions, not perfection
    • Ask: What do we need to learn? What assumptions should we test?
  • Test:
    • Get feedback from users
    • Observe how they interact with your prototype
    • Ask open-ended questions: What works? What doesn't? What would you change?
    • Use feedback to refine your problem definition and iterate

2. Understanding African Contexts

When designing for African communities, consider:

  • Access Constraints:
    • Limited internet connectivity
    • Limited access to components, materials, tools
    • Limited technical expertise
    • Design for offline operation, local materials, simple maintenance
  • Economic Constraints:
    • Limited purchasing power
    • Need for affordable solutions
    • Design for low cost, local manufacturing, scalability
  • Environmental Constraints:
    • Heat, dust, humidity
    • Variable power supply
    • Limited water access
    • Design for robustness, power efficiency, environmental resilience
  • Cultural Considerations:
    • Local languages and communication
    • Cultural practices and preferences
    • Social structures and community dynamics
    • Design with, not for—involve community in design process

3. Stakeholder Mapping

Identify all people affected by your solution:

  • Primary Users: People who will directly use your solution
  • Secondary Users: People indirectly affected (family, community)
  • Decision Makers: People who decide whether to adopt your solution
  • Manufacturers/Suppliers: People who will make or supply components
  • Maintainers: People who will maintain or repair your solution

For each stakeholder, understand: their needs, constraints, motivations, and how they'll interact with your solution.

4. Prototyping Techniques

Different prototyping methods for different purposes:

  • Paper Prototypes:
    • Fast, cheap, easy to modify
    • Use: Testing layouts, interfaces, form factors
    • Materials: Paper, cardboard, markers
  • Cardboard Prototypes:
    • 3D form, still fast and cheap
    • Use: Testing size, shape, ergonomics
    • Materials: Cardboard, tape, scissors
  • Digital Fabrication Prototypes:
    • 3D printed or laser cut parts
    • Use: Testing fit, function, aesthetics
    • Materials: PLA, cardboard, acrylic
  • Wizard of Oz Prototypes:
    • Fake functionality (human operates behind scenes)
    • Use: Testing user experience without building full system
    • Example: Fake "smart" device where human responds to inputs

Mini-Lab: Design Thinking Workshop

Let's work through a complete design thinking cycle for a real problem in your community.

Step 1: Empathize (30 minutes)

  1. Choose a problem area: Agriculture, health, education, energy, water, etc.
  2. Identify users: Who is affected by this problem?
  3. Observe and interview:
    • Spend time with users (if possible)
    • Ask open-ended questions: "Tell me about..." "What challenges do you face?"
    • Observe their behavior and environment
    • Take notes and photos (with permission)
  4. Document findings: Create empathy maps, user journey maps, or notes

Step 2: Define (20 minutes)

  1. Synthesize research: What patterns do you see? What are the core needs?
  2. Write problem statements:
    • From user perspective: "Users need X because Y"
    • Be specific, not vague
    • Example: "Farmers need to monitor soil moisture remotely because they can't visit fields daily"
  3. Identify constraints: Cost, access, environment, technical limitations

Step 3: Ideate (30 minutes)

  1. Brainstorm solutions:
    • Generate 20+ ideas (quantity over quality)
    • No judgment—all ideas are valid
    • Build on others' ideas
    • Think wild—constrain later
  2. Use techniques:
    • Mind mapping
    • "How might we..." questions
    • Sketching
    • Analogous inspiration (what works in other contexts?)
  3. Select top ideas: Choose 3-5 most promising ideas

Step 4: Prototype (1-2 hours)

  1. Choose one idea to prototype (or prototype multiple)
  2. Build low-fidelity prototype:
    • Paper/cardboard for form
    • 3D print or laser cut for fit/function
    • Focus on testing key assumptions
    • Don't make it perfect—make it testable
  3. Document prototype: Photos, notes on what it does/doesn't do

Step 5: Test (30 minutes)

  1. Show prototype to users (or potential users)
  2. Observe: How do they interact with it? What do they do?
  3. Ask questions:
    • "What do you think this does?"
    • "What works well?"
    • "What doesn't work?"
    • "What would you change?"
    • "Would you use this? Why or why not?"
  4. Document feedback: Take notes, photos, record insights

Step 6: Iterate

  1. Reflect on feedback: What did you learn?
  2. Refine problem definition: Did you understand the problem correctly?
  3. Plan improvements: What would you change in the next iteration?
  4. Document process: What worked? What didn't? What surprised you?

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Individual Assignment 1: Problem Documentation

Document at least one problem from your community and sketch three potential solutions:

  • Problem identification:
    • Choose a real problem you've observed in your community
    • Document through: observation, interviews, photos, notes
    • Identify stakeholders: Who is affected? Who would use a solution?
    • Define constraints: Cost, access, environment, technical limitations
  • Problem statement:
    • Write clear problem statement from user's perspective
    • Explain why this problem matters
    • Describe the context (African context, local conditions)
  • Three solution sketches:
    • Sketch three different approaches to solving the problem
    • Each sketch should show: what it is, how it works, key features
    • Include brief notes explaining each solution
    • Don't worry about feasibility yet—focus on ideas

Group Assignment

Choose one idea and build a quick physical prototype:

  • As a group, select one idea to prototype (can be from any group member)
  • Build a low-fidelity prototype using:
    • Paper and cardboard (fastest, most flexible)
    • Laser-cut parts (if you have access)
    • 3D-printed parts (if you have access)
    • Or combination of methods
  • Prototype should:
    • Test key assumptions about the solution
    • Be testable with users
    • Show form, function, or interaction (depending on what you're testing)
  • Document the prototype:
    • Photos of prototype
    • What it does and doesn't do
    • What assumptions you're testing

Individual Assignment 2: User Testing and Reflection

Reflect on user feedback and how you would iterate in later technical weeks:

  • Test your prototype with at least 2-3 potential users
  • Document feedback:
    • What did users like?
    • What didn't work?
    • What surprised you?
    • What would they change?
  • Reflect on the process:
    • What did you learn about the problem?
    • What did you learn about your solution?
    • How would you improve the prototype?
    • What technical skills would you need to build a real version?
  • Plan for future weeks:
    • How would you use skills from upcoming weeks to improve this?
    • What would you add (electronics, programming, networking, etc.)?
    • Could this become your final project?

Documentation Checklist

Item Description
Problem Documentation Clear description of the problem, who is affected, why it matters, and the context. Include photos, interview notes, or observation records.
Stakeholder Map Diagram or list showing all stakeholders, their needs, constraints, and relationships.
Problem Statement Clear, user-centered problem statement: "Users need X because Y"
Three Solution Sketches Three different approaches to solving the problem, each with:
  • Sketch showing the solution
  • Brief description
  • Key features
  • How it addresses the problem
Prototype Photos Photos of your physical prototype from multiple angles, showing what it does.
User Testing Documentation Notes from user testing:
  • Who you tested with
  • What you observed
  • Feedback received
  • Photos of users interacting with prototype (with permission)
Reflection Written reflection on:
  • What you learned about the problem
  • What you learned about your solution
  • How you would improve the prototype
  • What technical skills you'd need for a real version
  • How this could evolve in later weeks
Remember: Design thinking is iterative. Don't expect to get it right the first time. The goal is to learn about the problem and your users, not to create a perfect solution immediately.

Tips for Success

  • Start with real problems: Choose problems you've actually observed, not hypothetical ones.
  • Talk to users: Don't assume you know what users need—ask them, observe them.
  • Generate many ideas: Don't settle on the first solution—brainstorm many options.
  • Prototype quickly: Don't overthink—build something testable, even if it's rough.
  • Test early and often: Get feedback as soon as possible, not at the end.
  • Embrace failure: Failed prototypes teach you more than successful ones.
  • Think in context: Consider African constraints (cost, access, environment) from the start.
  • Document everything: Photos, notes, sketches—you'll need this for future iterations.
  • Be open to change: Be willing to change your problem definition based on what you learn.
  • Think long-term: How could this evolve into your final project?
