Wildcard Week

Empowering Africa's Future with the UNIPOD Digital Fabrication Program.
In partnership with Timbuktoo, UNDP, FabLab Rwanda and UNIPODS, we're delivering cutting-edge technology education across Africa.

Overview

Welcome to Wildcard Week! This is your opportunity to explore a technology or process that isn't fully covered elsewhere in the course. Choose something that interests you, is emerging, or is uniquely relevant to your UNIPOD or community.

The digital fabrication landscape is constantly evolving, and every region has unique techniques, materials, and challenges. This week encourages you to explore beyond the standard curriculum—whether it's biomaterials, advanced composites, robotics, textiles, or a local technique you want to document.

This week is about deep exploration and creating a tutorial that others can follow. By the end, you'll have documented a new skill or technique that adds to the collective knowledge of African makers. Your work will help build a repository of techniques that are relevant, accessible, and useful in African contexts.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Identify a technology or technique that interests you and is relevant to your context
  • Research and learn a new skill or process independently
  • Experiment and prototype using the new technique
  • Document comprehensively as a tutorial that others can follow
  • Reflect on applications of the technique in African contexts
  • Share knowledge with the UNIPOD community

Possible Wildcard Topics

1. Biomaterials and Sustainable Materials

Explore materials made from biological sources or waste:

  • Mycelium materials: Growing structures from mushroom roots
  • Bioplastics: Creating plastics from plant materials
  • Natural fiber composites: Combining local fibers (sisal, jute) with resins
  • Waste material upcycling: Turning local waste into useful materials
  • Applications: Packaging, construction materials, consumer products

2. Advanced Composites and Textiles

Explore composite materials and textile fabrication:

  • Carbon fiber or fiberglass: Creating strong, lightweight structures
  • Textile electronics: Integrating circuits into fabrics
  • 3D printing with composites: Printing with fiber-reinforced materials
  • Applications: Lightweight structures, wearable technology, protective equipment

3. Robotics and Machine Building

Build custom machines or robots:

  • CNC machine construction: Building your own CNC router or mill
  • Robot arms: Creating robotic manipulators
  • Automated systems: Building machines for specific tasks
  • Applications: Automation, manufacturing, research tools

4. Local Techniques and Traditional Crafts

Document and modernize local techniques:

  • Traditional pottery or ceramics: Documenting local techniques
  • Basket weaving: Combining traditional and digital techniques
  • Local material processing: Techniques for working with local materials
  • Applications: Preserving knowledge, creating hybrid techniques, local entrepreneurship

5. Emerging Technologies

Explore cutting-edge technologies:

  • Machine learning for makers: Using ML in embedded projects
  • Blockchain for supply chains: Tracking materials and products
  • Advanced 3D printing: Multi-material, metal printing, etc.
  • Applications: Innovation, research, future technologies

Mini-Lab: Choose and Explore Your Wildcard Topic

Let's structure your wildcard exploration to ensure you create valuable documentation.

Step 1: Choose Your Topic

Select something that:

  • Interests you personally
  • Is relevant to your community or UNIPOD
  • Is feasible with available resources
  • Can be learned and documented in one week
  • Adds value to the UNIPOD community

Step 2: Research

Gather information:

  • Find existing tutorials, papers, or documentation
  • Identify required materials and tools
  • Understand safety considerations
  • Find examples or case studies

Step 3: Plan Your Experiment

Define what you'll do:

  • What specific experiment or prototype will you create?
  • What materials and tools do you need?
  • What are the key steps?
  • What will you measure or test?

Step 4: Execute and Document

As you work:

  • Take photos at each step
  • Record settings, parameters, and measurements
  • Note what works and what doesn't
  • Document failures and how you fixed them

Step 5: Create Tutorial

Structure your documentation as a tutorial:

  • Introduction: What is this technique? Why is it useful?
  • Materials and tools: Complete list with sources
  • Step-by-step process: Clear instructions with photos
  • Parameters and settings: Exact values used
  • Results: What you achieved, photos of outcomes
  • Lessons learned: What worked, what didn't, improvements
  • Applications: How could this be used in African contexts?

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Individual or Group Assignment

Choose a wildcard topic, run at least one experiment/prototype, and document it as a tutorial others can follow:

  • Select a topic that:
    • Isn't fully covered in other weeks
    • Interests you and is relevant to your context
    • Is feasible with available resources
  • Run at least one experiment or create one prototype
  • Document the process comprehensively as a tutorial
  • Reflect on how this technique could serve African makers

Documentation Checklist

Item Description
Introduction and Motivation Clear explanation of what the technique is, why it's useful, and why you chose it. Include context about relevance to African makers.
Materials and Tools Complete list of all materials and tools needed, with:
  • Specific names and part numbers
  • Quantities required
  • Where to source (especially local sources)
  • Approximate costs
Step-by-Step Process Detailed instructions with:
  • Clear step numbering
  • Photos at each major step
  • Warnings for safety-critical steps
  • Troubleshooting tips
Parameters and Settings Document all important parameters:
  • Exact settings used (temperatures, speeds, times, etc.)
  • File parameters (if using digital tools)
  • Any calculations or formulas
Results Show what you achieved:
  • Photos of final results
  • Measurements or test results
  • Comparison with expectations
Files and Resources Share all files others can reuse:
  • CAD files, code, configuration files
  • Links to external resources
  • References to papers or tutorials you used
Lessons Learned Reflection on:
  • What worked well
  • What didn't work and why
  • What you would do differently
  • Common mistakes to avoid
Applications for African Makers Reflection on:
  • How could this technique be used in African contexts?
  • What problems could it solve?
  • What adaptations might be needed?
  • What business or social impact opportunities exist?
Remember: Your tutorial should be clear enough that someone else in another UNIPOD could follow it and reproduce your work. Include enough detail but keep it readable and well-organized.

Tips for Success

  • Choose something achievable: Pick a topic you can realistically explore and document in one week.
  • Start with research: Learn from existing resources before experimenting.
  • Document as you go: Take photos and notes during the process, not after.
  • Test and iterate: Don't expect perfection on the first try—document your learning process.
  • Think locally: Consider how the technique could be adapted for local materials, conditions, and needs.
  • Share knowledge: Your tutorial helps build the collective knowledge of African makers.
  • Be thorough: Include enough detail that others can reproduce your work.
  • Reflect on impact: Think about how this technique could create value in your community.
