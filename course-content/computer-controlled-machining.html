Overview

Welcome to Computer-Controlled Machining! This week, you'll learn to use large-format CNC routers to create furniture, signage, and other big objects. CNC machining enables you to work with full sheets of plywood, MDF, or other materials, creating strong, precise assemblies.

In African contexts, CNC machining enables local production of furniture, construction elements, and large-scale objects that would be expensive to import. Whether you're making classroom furniture for schools, storage for labs, or signage for communities, CNC machining gives you the ability to create substantial, functional objects from locally available materials.

We'll focus on designing for CNC (press-fit joints, toolpath considerations), using CAM software to generate safe G-code, and operating large machines safely. By the end of the week, you'll have created a substantial object that addresses a local need—whether it's classroom furniture, lab storage, or community signage.

Core Concepts & Theory

1. Understanding CNC Routers

CNC (Computer Numerical Control) routers are machines that cut materials using computer-controlled movements:

  • Spindle: Rotating motor that holds cutting tools (end mills, router bits)
  • Gantry: Moving frame that positions the spindle in X, Y, and Z axes
  • Bed/Table: Surface where material is secured
  • Controller: Computer that interprets G-code and controls machine movements
  • Tool: Cutting bit (end mill) that removes material

Types of operations:

  • Profile cuts: Cutting along the outline of a shape (through-cut or partial depth)
  • Pocket cuts: Removing material from inside a shape
  • Drilling: Creating holes
  • Engraving: Shallow cuts for text or patterns

2. Toolpaths and Cutting Strategies

Toolpaths define how the tool moves to cut your design:

  • Climb vs. Conventional Milling:
    • Climb milling: Tool rotates in direction of travel (better finish, less tool wear)
    • Conventional milling: Tool rotates against direction of travel (more stable, better for rough cuts)
    • Most CAM software defaults to climb milling for finish passes
  • Stepover: Distance between parallel toolpaths (typically 50-80% of tool diameter)
  • Stepdown: Depth of each pass (typically 1-2× tool diameter, or less for hard materials)
  • Feed rate: How fast the tool moves (mm/min or inches/min)
  • Spindle speed: How fast the tool rotates (RPM)

3. Feeds and Speeds

Proper feeds and speeds are critical for quality cuts and tool life:

  • Feed Rate (mm/min):
    • Too slow: Tool rubs, burns material, poor finish
    • Too fast: Tool breaks, poor finish, machine strain
    • Typical: 1000-3000 mm/min for plywood with 6mm end mill
  • Spindle Speed (RPM):
    • Too slow: Poor finish, tool wear
    • Too fast: Tool breaks, burns material
    • Typical: 12000-18000 RPM for plywood
  • Depth of Cut:
    • Too deep: Tool breaks, poor finish
    • Too shallow: Slow cutting, tool wear
    • Typical: 1-3mm per pass for plywood (depending on tool size)
  • Calculating Feeds and Speeds:
    • Use online calculators or CAM software recommendations
    • Start conservative and increase gradually
    • Test cuts are essential—every machine and material is different

4. Material Holding

Securing material is critical for safety and accuracy:

  • Screws:
    • Screw material to waste board or table
    • Place screws in areas that will be cut away
    • Use enough screws to prevent movement
  • Clamps:
    • Use clamps around edges (outside cut area)
    • Ensure clamps don't interfere with toolpath
    • Check clearance before starting
  • Vacuum Table (if available):
    • Holds material with suction
    • No screws needed, but requires good seal
    • Check vacuum pressure before cutting
  • Double-Sided Tape (for small parts):
    • Use strong tape for light cuts
    • Not suitable for heavy cutting forces

5. Designing for CNC

Design considerations for CNC machining:

  • Tool Size Constraints:
    • Minimum inside corner radius = tool radius (can't cut sharp inside corners)
    • Minimum slot width = tool diameter + clearance
    • Design for available tool sizes (typically 3mm, 6mm, 12mm end mills)
  • Press-Fit Joints:
    • Slots should be slightly smaller than material thickness (account for kerf)
    • Test fit with test cuts before final design
    • Typical clearance: Material thickness - 0.1mm to 0.2mm (depends on material and tool)
  • Tab and Slot Joints:
    • Strong, no fasteners needed
    • Design tabs slightly smaller than slots for press-fit
    • Add chamfers or fillets to ease assembly
  • Material Considerations:
    • Design for available sheet sizes (typically 1220×2440mm or 4×8 feet)
    • Minimize waste by nesting parts efficiently
    • Consider material thickness variations (measure actual material)

Mini-Lab: Characterize Your CNC Router

Before designing your project, let's characterize your CNC router to understand its capabilities and optimal settings.

Step 1: Create Test Cut Design

Design a test piece that explores:

  • Different slot widths (3mm, 6mm, 9mm, 12mm)
  • Different depths (1mm, 2mm, 3mm, through-cut)
  • Inside corners (sharp, 3mm radius, 6mm radius)
  • Text at different sizes (5mm, 10mm, 20mm height)
  • Circles of different sizes (10mm, 20mm, 50mm diameter)

Step 2: Test Different Tools

Cut test pieces with different tools:

  • 3mm end mill (fine detail, slower)
  • 6mm end mill (balanced, most common)
  • 12mm end mill (fast, rough cuts)

For each tool, test different feed rates and spindle speeds.

Step 3: Test Material Holding

Test different holding methods:

  • Screws in waste areas
  • Clamps around edges
  • Vacuum (if available)

Check which method works best for your material and machine.

Step 4: Measure Kerf

Kerf is the width of material removed by the cut:

  1. Cut a slot (e.g., 10mm wide)
  2. Measure actual slot width
  3. Kerf = Actual width - Tool diameter
  4. Example: 10mm slot measures 10.2mm with 6mm tool → Kerf = 0.2mm

Use kerf value in your designs for accurate press-fit joints.

Step 5: Document Results

Create a table documenting:

  • Tool size
  • Feed rate and spindle speed
  • Material
  • Cut quality (smooth, rough, burned)
  • Kerf measurement
  • Best settings for your machine

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Group Assignment

Characterize your CNC router:

  • Run test cuts exploring:
    • Different tools (3mm, 6mm, 12mm end mills)
    • Different feed rates (slow, medium, fast)
    • Different spindle speeds
    • Different materials (plywood, MDF, acrylic if available)
  • Document results:
    • Create a table comparing settings and results
    • Note cut quality (smooth, rough, burned, chipped)
    • Measure kerf for each tool
    • Identify optimal settings for your machine
  • Share findings:
    • Create a shared document or wiki
    • Include photos of test cuts
    • Note any machine-specific quirks or limitations

Individual Assignment

Design and fabricate a big object that uses press-fit or mechanical joints:

  • Design requirements:
    • Use full sheet material (or significant portion)
    • Include press-fit joints (tabs and slots)
    • Design for available material thickness
    • Account for kerf in joint design
    • Make it parametric (so dimensions can be adjusted)
  • Fabrication:
    • Generate toolpaths using CAM software
    • Review toolpaths for safety (no collisions, proper depths)
    • Cut on CNC router
    • Assemble using press-fit joints (no screws/glue if possible)
  • Reflection:
    • How does your design address a local need?
    • What would you change if making it again?
    • How could this be scaled or adapted?

Safety First: CNC routers are powerful machines. Always:
  • Wear safety glasses and hearing protection
  • Follow lab safety protocols
  • Never leave machine unattended while cutting
  • Know where emergency stop is located
  • Check toolpaths carefully before cutting
  • Start with test cuts at low speeds

Tips for Success

  • Characterize first: Do test cuts before designing your project—know your machine's capabilities.
  • Measure actual material: Material thickness can vary. Measure your actual sheets, don't assume.
  • Account for kerf: Test and measure kerf for each tool. Use this in your joint designs.
  • Start conservative: Use slower feeds/speeds initially, then increase if cuts are good.
  • Check toolpaths: Review toolpaths in CAM software before cutting. Look for collisions, wrong depths, etc.
  • Secure material well: Material movement during cutting is dangerous and ruins cuts.
  • Test joints first: Cut a small test piece with your joint design before cutting the full project.
  • Design for assembly: Add chamfers or fillets to make assembly easier.
  • Nest efficiently: Arrange parts on sheet to minimize waste.
  • Document everything: Record settings, tool choices, and results—you'll need this for future projects.
