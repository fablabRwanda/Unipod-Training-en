Overview — 3D Scanning & Printing

Welcome to 3D Scanning and Printing! This week, you'll learn to capture real‑world objects using low‑cost tools (phones, webcams, handheld scanners) and turn them into printable 3D models. You'll also learn to design for 3D printing and operate FDM printers safely to create complex geometries that would be difficult with traditional manufacturing.

Learning Objectives

  • Compare at least two 3D scanning workflows (e.g. photogrammetry vs. handheld scanner or phone app).
  • Process scans into clean meshes ready for fabrication (repair, simplify, scale).
  • Design or adapt a 3D model that respects your printer’s design rules (overhangs, wall thickness, tolerances).
  • Document your full pipeline so someone in another African UNIPOD can reproduce your work.

Assignments

  • Group: Characterize your 3D printer by printing test pieces that explore overhangs, bridges, clearance and dimensional accuracy on the filament you use most.
  • Individual: 3D scan an object from your environment (e.g. traditional craft, everyday tool, natural form), process the scan into a watertight mesh and prepare it for printing.
  • Individual: Design and print a small object that cannot be made subtractively (e.g. internal channels, captive parts, complex organic shape).

1. 3D Scanning Workflows

Start with tools you already have. Many African labs and UNIPODs may not have expensive industrial scanners, but you can still do powerful work using phones, webcams, or low‑cost depth sensors.

Photogrammetry (Images → 3D)

  • Take 30–80 photos of your object from all around, keeping lighting as constant as possible.
  • Use a photogrammetry tool (desktop or cloud) to generate a dense point cloud and then a mesh.
  • Clean up the mesh by cropping the ground plane and removing noise.

Best for: Cultural artifacts, organic shapes, surfaces with good texture.

Tip: Avoid shiny or fully black objects; add tape or powder if needed.

Depth / Handheld Scanners

  • Follow your device’s recommended scanning path (circle around, move steadily).
  • Ensure enough overlap between frames so the software can track surfaces reliably.
  • Export as .obj or .stl for further cleanup.

Best for: People, large objects, quick captures when you have access to a scanner.

Tip: Practice with simple shapes first (bottles, boxes) to learn how your scanner behaves.

2. Mesh Cleanup & Preparing for Print

Raw scans are rarely ready to print. This block focuses on transforming noisy meshes into printable geometry.

  • Repair: Close holes, remove floating islands, ensure the mesh is watertight.
  • Simplify: Reduce polygon count while preserving important details so slicers and low‑spec laptops can handle your model.
  • Scale & orient: Measure the real object and set the correct scale; choose an orientation that reduces supports and preserves key surfaces.
  • Combine with CAD: Use your scan as a reference to design add‑ons or enclosures (e.g. a custom phone mount or handle).

3. 3D Printer Characterization & Design Rules

As a group, you will run printer characterization tests to understand what your machines can do:

  • Print an overhang/bridge test and record the maximum angle/length that prints cleanly.
  • Print a clearance test (moving parts, gaps) and note the smallest gap that moves freely.
  • Measure real dimensions vs. CAD (X, Y, Z) and document any systematic errors.

Use these results to define “design rules” for your UNIPOD (minimum wall thickness, minimum hole size, recommended layer height) and publish them for future cohorts.

4. Documentation & Recommended Tutorials

What You Must Document

  • Your scanning setup (device, lighting, number of photos or scan time).
  • Screenshots from each major step: raw scan, cleaned mesh, print in slicer, final print.
  • Printer settings table (material, nozzle temp, bed temp, layer height, infill, speed).
  • Reflections: what failed, what you changed, and how you would improve next time.
