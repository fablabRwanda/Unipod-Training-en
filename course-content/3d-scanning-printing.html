Overview

Welcome to 3D Scanning and Printing! This week, you'll learn to capture real‑world objects using low‑cost tools—especially your phone for photogrammetry—and turn them into printable 3D models. You'll also learn to design for 3D printing and operate FDM printers safely to create complex geometries that would be difficult with traditional manufacturing.

In African contexts, where expensive 3D scanners may not be available, phone-based photogrammetry opens powerful possibilities. Using just your smartphone camera, you can scan cultural artifacts, everyday objects, natural forms, and more. This technique is perfect for preserving traditional crafts, creating custom-fit parts, or digitizing objects for reproduction.

This week builds on Computer-Aided Design (where you learned 3D modeling) and prepares you for Final Project (where you might need to scan reference objects or print custom parts). By the end of the week, you'll understand how to scan objects with your phone, process scans into printable models, and design parts that leverage 3D printing's unique capabilities.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Perform photogrammetry scanning using your phone to capture objects from multiple angles
  • Process photogrammetry data into 3D meshes using desktop or cloud software
  • Compare scanning methods including photogrammetry, handheld scanners, and phone apps
  • Clean and repair meshes to make them watertight and printable
  • Design for 3D printing respecting overhangs, wall thickness, tolerances, and support requirements
  • Characterize 3D printers through test prints to understand capabilities and limitations
  • Operate FDM printers safely including setup, slicing, and troubleshooting
  • Document scanning and printing workflows so others can reproduce your process

Core Concepts & Theory

1. Photogrammetry: 3D Scanning with Your Phone

Photogrammetry is a technique that creates 3D models from multiple photographs. It's perfect for African contexts because it requires only a smartphone—no expensive equipment needed!

How it works:

  • Take 30-100 photos of an object from different angles
  • Software analyzes the photos to find common points (features)
  • Triangulation calculates 3D positions of these points
  • Creates a dense point cloud, then a mesh (3D surface)

Why photogrammetry is ideal for African contexts:

  • Accessible: Works with any smartphone camera
  • Free/affordable: Many free software options available
  • Portable: Can scan objects anywhere
  • High quality: Can capture fine details and textures
  • No special equipment: No need for structured light scanners or depth cameras

2. Photogrammetry Best Practices

To get good results with phone photogrammetry:

  • Lighting:
    • Use consistent, diffuse lighting (overcast day, or indoor with soft light)
    • Avoid harsh shadows and direct sunlight
    • Keep lighting constant throughout the photo session
  • Object preparation:
    • Choose objects with texture (helps software match features)
    • Avoid shiny, reflective, or transparent objects (or add matte spray/powder)
    • Avoid pure black or pure white objects (add markers or texture)
    • Place object on textured surface or add markers around it
  • Photo technique:
    • Take 50-100 photos covering all angles
    • Move in a circle around the object, taking photos every 10-15 degrees
    • Take photos at different heights (top, middle, bottom views)
    • Ensure 60-80% overlap between adjacent photos
    • Keep camera focused and steady (avoid blur)
    • Use consistent distance from object (1-2 meters for small objects)
  • Camera settings:
    • Use highest resolution available on your phone
    • Turn off flash (creates inconsistent lighting)
    • Use manual focus if available (lock focus on object)
    • Keep exposure consistent (use manual mode if available)

3. Photogrammetry Software Options

Choose software based on your resources:

  • Cloud-based (Requires Internet):
    • RealityCapture: Professional quality, free for non-commercial use (limited exports)
    • 3DF Zephyr: Good quality, free version available
    • Agisoft Metashape: Professional, paid but has trial
    • Pros: Powerful, no local processing needed
    • Cons: Requires good internet, may have usage limits
  • Desktop Software (Works Offline):
    • Meshroom: Free, open-source, works offline
    • COLMAP: Free, open-source, command-line based
    • OpenMVG + OpenMVS: Free, open-source pipeline
    • Pros: Works offline, no usage limits, full control
    • Cons: Requires powerful computer, more complex setup
  • Phone Apps (Direct Scanning):
    • Polycam: iOS/Android, uses LiDAR (if available) or photogrammetry
    • Scaniverse: iOS, photogrammetry-based
    • 3D Scanner App: Android, various methods
    • Pros: Very easy, works directly on phone
    • Cons: May have quality limitations, some apps are paid

4. Other 3D Scanning Methods

Alternative methods if photogrammetry isn't suitable:

  • Handheld 3D Scanners:
    • Structured light or laser scanners
    • Pros: Fast, real-time feedback, good for people/large objects
    • Cons: Expensive, may not be available in all labs
  • Depth Cameras:
    • Intel RealSense, Microsoft Kinect
    • Pros: Real-time scanning, good for large objects
    • Cons: Limited resolution, may not be available
  • LiDAR (on newer phones):
    • iPhone 12 Pro and later, some Android phones
    • Pros: Very fast, good for rooms/large objects
    • Cons: Limited to phones with LiDAR, lower detail than photogrammetry

5. Mesh Processing and Cleanup

Raw scans need processing before printing:

  • Repair:
    • Close holes (meshlab, Blender, MeshMixer)
    • Remove floating islands (unconnected geometry)
    • Ensure mesh is watertight (no gaps, closed surface)
    • Fix non-manifold edges (edges shared by more than 2 faces)
  • Simplify:
    • Reduce polygon count (decimation)
    • Keep important details, remove noise
    • Target: 50,000-200,000 triangles for printing (depends on object size)
    • Too many polygons: Slow slicing, large files
    • Too few polygons: Loss of detail
  • Scale and Orient:
    • Measure real object dimensions
    • Scale mesh to match real size
    • Orient for printing (minimize supports, preserve important surfaces)
  • Combine with CAD:
    • Import scan into CAD software
    • Use as reference to design custom parts
    • Example: Scan a phone, design a custom case that fits perfectly

6. Design for 3D Printing (DFAM)

3D printing has unique capabilities and constraints:

  • Additive Manufacturing Advantages:
    • Complex internal structures (channels, lattices)
    • Organic shapes difficult to machine
    • Customization (parametric designs)
    • Assembly-free parts (captive joints, hinges)
  • Design Constraints:
    • Overhangs: Angles >45° need supports (test your printer's limit)
    • Bridging: Horizontal spans need supports if too long (test your printer)
    • Wall thickness: Minimum 2× nozzle diameter (typically 0.8-1.2mm)
    • Clearance: Moving parts need gap (typically 0.2-0.5mm, test your printer)
    • Hole size: Small holes may close up (test minimum size)
    • Layer height: Affects surface quality and print time

Mini-Lab: Phone Photogrammetry Scanning

Let's scan an object using your phone and photogrammetry—no expensive equipment needed!

Step 1: Choose and Prepare Your Object

  1. Select an object:
    • Start with something small (10-30cm) and textured
    • Good choices: Traditional craft, tool, toy, decorative object, natural form
    • Avoid: Shiny metal, glass, transparent objects, pure black/white
  2. Prepare the object:
    • If shiny: Add matte spray, powder, or tape to reduce reflections
    • If smooth/featureless: Add texture markers (small stickers, tape)
    • Clean the object (remove dust, fingerprints)
  3. Set up environment:
    • Place object on textured surface (wood, fabric, paper with pattern)
    • Use diffuse lighting (overcast day, or indoor with soft light from multiple directions)
    • Remove background clutter or add markers around object

Step 2: Take Photos with Your Phone

  1. Camera settings:
    • Use highest resolution available
    • Turn off flash
    • Use manual focus if available (lock focus on object)
    • Keep exposure consistent
  2. Photo technique:
    • Take 50-100 photos total
    • Move in a circle around object, taking photos every 10-15 degrees
    • Take photos at 3-4 different heights:
      • Top view (looking down)
      • Eye level (horizontal)
      • Low angle (looking up)
      • Bottom view (if accessible)
    • Ensure 60-80% overlap between adjacent photos
    • Keep consistent distance (1-2 meters for small objects)
    • Keep camera steady, avoid blur
  3. Check your photos:
    • Review photos to ensure they're in focus
    • Ensure good coverage (all angles captured)
    • Delete blurry or poorly lit photos

Step 3: Process Photos with Photogrammetry Software

Option A: Cloud-based (Requires Internet)

  1. Upload photos to cloud service (RealityCapture, 3DF Zephyr, etc.)
  2. Software automatically:
    • Detects features in photos
    • Matches features between photos
    • Calculates camera positions
    • Creates point cloud
    • Generates mesh
  3. Wait for processing (can take 30 minutes to several hours depending on number of photos)
  4. Download resulting mesh

Option B: Desktop Software (Works Offline)

  1. Install Meshroom (free, open-source) or similar
  2. Import photos into software
  3. Run processing pipeline:
    • Feature detection
    • Feature matching
    • Structure from motion
    • Dense reconstruction
    • Mesh generation
  4. Export mesh as OBJ or STL

Step 4: Clean and Repair Mesh

  1. Import into mesh editing software (Meshlab, Blender, MeshMixer)
  2. Remove unwanted parts:
    • Delete ground plane
    • Remove background objects
    • Crop to object of interest
  3. Repair mesh:
    • Close holes (Fill Holes tool)
    • Remove floating islands
    • Fix non-manifold edges
    • Ensure watertight mesh
  4. Simplify if needed:
    • Reduce polygon count (decimation)
    • Target: 50,000-200,000 triangles
    • Preserve important details
  5. Scale to real size:
    • Measure real object dimensions
    • Scale mesh to match

Step 5: Prepare for 3D Printing

  1. Import into slicer (Cura, PrusaSlicer, etc.)
  2. Orient model:
    • Minimize overhangs
    • Reduce support material
    • Preserve important surfaces
  3. Check for issues:
    • Verify model is watertight
    • Check for thin walls (may need to thicken)
    • Ensure minimum feature size is printable
  4. Slice and print:
    • Generate G-code
    • Review layer preview
    • Print and observe results

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Group Assignment

Characterize your 3D printer:

  • Print test pieces exploring:
    • Overhangs (test angles from 30° to 90°)
    • Bridges (test spans of different lengths)
    • Clearance (test gaps between moving parts: 0.1mm, 0.2mm, 0.3mm, etc.)
    • Dimensional accuracy (print calibration cube, measure X, Y, Z dimensions)
  • Document results:
    • Create table showing: test type, settings used, results, best values
    • Photos of test prints
    • Define "design rules" for your UNIPOD:
      • Maximum overhang angle
      • Maximum bridge length
      • Minimum clearance for moving parts
      • Minimum wall thickness
      • Recommended layer height
  • Share findings with your UNIPOD for future reference

Individual Assignment 1: Phone Photogrammetry Scan

3D scan an object using phone photogrammetry, process it, and prepare for printing:

  • Choose an object from your environment:
    • Traditional craft, everyday tool, natural form, cultural artifact
    • Something meaningful to you or your community
  • Scan using phone photogrammetry:
    • Take 50-100 photos following best practices
    • Process photos using photogrammetry software (Meshroom, RealityCapture, or phone app)
    • Document your photo-taking process (lighting, setup, technique)
  • Process the scan:
    • Clean and repair mesh (close holes, remove noise)
    • Simplify if needed
    • Scale to real size
    • Export as STL or OBJ
  • Prepare for printing:
    • Import into slicer
    • Orient for printing
    • Generate G-code (you don't need to print it, but show it's ready)

Individual Assignment 2: Design and Print Non-Subtractive Object

Design and print a small object that cannot be made subtractively:

  • Design requirements:
    • Must have features impossible with subtractive manufacturing:
      • Internal channels or cavities
      • Captive parts (parts trapped inside)
      • Complex organic shapes
      • Overhangs or undercuts
    • Respect your printer's design rules (from group characterization)
    • Keep it small (to save material and time)
  • Print the object:
    • Slice with appropriate settings
    • Print successfully
    • Document any failures and how you fixed them

Tips for Success

  • Start simple: Begin with textured, matte objects before trying challenging materials.
  • Take many photos: More photos = better results. 50-100 photos is a good target.
  • Ensure overlap: Adjacent photos should share 60-80% of their content.
  • Watch lighting: Consistent, diffuse lighting is crucial for good photogrammetry.
  • Test your printer: Do characterization tests before designing complex parts.
  • Design for your printer: Use the design rules from your characterization tests.
  • Clean meshes well: Spend time repairing and cleaning—it makes printing much easier.
  • Scale accurately: Measure real objects and scale meshes to match.
  • Orient wisely: Choose print orientation to minimize supports and preserve important surfaces.
  • Document everything: Record settings, techniques, and results—you'll need this for future projects.
