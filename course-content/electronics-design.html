Overview

Welcome to Electronics Design! This week, you'll learn to design your own printed circuit boards (PCBs) using Electronic Design Automation (EDA) software. Whether you're building a sensor node for agriculture, a controller for a water pump, or a custom board for your final project, understanding PCB design is essential for creating reliable, professional electronics.

In many African contexts, importing ready-made development boards can be expensive and slow. By learning to design your own boards, you gain independence to create exactly what you need, using components available locally. We'll focus on practical skills: drawing schematics, routing traces, and preparing files for fabrication in your UNIPOD lab.

This week builds directly on Electronics Production (where you learned to mill and solder boards) and prepares you for Embedded Programming (where you'll write code for your custom hardware). By the end, you'll have designed a microcontroller board with at least one input and one output device, ready for fabrication and programming.

Learning Outcomes

By the end of this week, you will be able to:

  • Draw schematics using EDA software, connecting microcontrollers, power supplies, and peripheral components correctly
  • Route PCB layouts with appropriate trace widths, clearances, and via placement for reliable fabrication
  • Use design rules (DRC) and electrical rules (ERC) to catch errors before sending files to production
  • Export fabrication files (Gerber, drill files, or PNG) compatible with your lab's PCB milling or production workflow
  • Select components based on availability, cost, and performance requirements in your local market
  • Document your design process so others can understand your choices and reproduce your work

Core Concepts & Theory

1. Understanding Schematics

A schematic is a symbolic representation of your circuit. Unlike a physical layout, it shows what connects to what, not where components sit. Think of it as a map of electrical relationships. Common symbols include:

  • Microcontrollers: Represented as rectangles with labeled pins (VCC, GND, GPIO pins, etc.)
  • Resistors: Zigzag lines or rectangles with values (e.g., 10kΩ, 220Ω)
  • Capacitors: Parallel lines (polarized) or curved lines (non-polarized)
  • Power symbols: VCC (positive), GND (ground), often shown as labels or symbols
  • Connectors: Headers, USB ports, or other interfaces your board will use

When drawing schematics, always include a power supply section (voltage regulator, decoupling capacitors), a microcontroller section, and your input/output peripherals. Label everything clearly—future you will thank present you!

2. From Schematic to PCB Layout

Once your schematic is complete and passes ERC (Electrical Rule Check), you move to the board layout. Here, you place physical components and route copper traces between them. Key considerations:

  • Component placement: Group related components together. Keep power components away from sensitive analog circuits. Place connectors near board edges for easy access.
  • Trace width: Power traces (VCC, GND) should be wider (e.g., 0.5mm) than signal traces (0.2mm). This reduces resistance and prevents voltage drops.
  • Clearances: Maintain minimum spacing between traces (typically 0.2mm for 1/64" end mills). This prevents short circuits during milling.
  • Vias: Small holes plated with copper, used to route traces between layers. For single-layer boards (common in labs), avoid vias or use wire jumpers.
  • Ground planes: Large areas of copper connected to GND improve signal integrity and make routing easier. Fill unused space with ground planes when possible.

3. Design Rules for Lab Fabrication

When designing for in-lab PCB milling (using machines like the SRM-20 or similar), you must account for the tool's limitations:

  • Minimum trace width: Typically 0.2mm (8 mils) for 1/64" end mills. Thinner traces may break during milling.
  • Minimum clearance: 0.2mm between traces. Tighter spacing risks shorts.
  • Via size: If using vias, make them at least 0.3mm diameter. Smaller vias are hard to drill reliably.
  • Component spacing: Leave room for hand-soldering. SMD components need space for your iron tip.
  • Text and labels: Use large, clear text (at least 1mm height) for component labels and board names.

Most EDA tools have Design Rule Check (DRC) settings where you can configure these limits. Always run DRC before exporting fabrication files!

4. Component Selection for African Markets

When choosing components, consider:

  • Availability: Can you source this component locally or from regional suppliers? Check with your lab manager or local electronics shops.
  • Cost: Microcontrollers like ATtiny44/45 or ATmega328P are affordable and widely available. Avoid exotic or very new parts unless necessary.
  • Package type: Through-hole components are easier to solder by hand, but SMD (Surface Mount) parts are smaller and cheaper. Start with through-hole if you're new to soldering.
  • Voltage compatibility: Many African power systems use 12V or 24V. Design your board to accept a range of input voltages with appropriate regulators (e.g., LM7805 for 5V, AMS1117 for 3.3V).

Mini-Lab: Design a Simple LED Controller Board

Let's design a practical board step-by-step: a microcontroller-based LED controller that can dim LEDs using PWM (Pulse Width Modulation).

Step 1: Define Requirements

  • Microcontroller: ATtiny44 (8 pins, enough for this project)
  • Input: One button to cycle through brightness levels
  • Output: Three LEDs with current-limiting resistors
  • Power: 5V via USB or external supply
  • Programming: ISP header for programming (6 pins)

Step 2: Draw the Schematic

  1. Open your EDA tool (KiCad, Eagle, or Fusion 360 Electronics).
  2. Add the ATtiny44 component. Place it in the center of your schematic.
  3. Add power components:
    • Voltage regulator (e.g., AMS1117-5.0) with input capacitor (10µF) and output capacitor (10µF)
    • Connect VCC to pin 1 (VCC) and pin 14 (AVCC) of ATtiny44
    • Connect GND to pin 4 (GND) and pin 8 (AGND)
  4. Add programming header (6-pin ISP):
    • Connect MISO, MOSI, SCK, RST, VCC, GND to corresponding ATtiny44 pins
  5. Add button input:
    • Button connected between PA7 (pin 6) and GND
    • Pull-up resistor (10kΩ) from PA7 to VCC
  6. Add LED outputs:
    • Three LEDs, each with a 220Ω resistor in series
    • Connect to PA0, PA1, PA2 (pins 13, 12, 11)
    • LEDs connect: VCC → Resistor → LED anode → LED cathode → GND
  7. Add decoupling capacitor: 100nF ceramic capacitor between VCC and GND, close to the microcontroller

Step 3: Create the Board Layout

  1. Switch to the board editor. Your components will appear as footprints.
  2. Place components:
    • Put the ISP header near the edge for easy access
    • Group LEDs together on one side
    • Place the button on the opposite side
    • Keep the voltage regulator and capacitors near the power input
  3. Route traces:
    • Start with power traces (VCC and GND)—make these wider (0.5mm)
    • Route signal traces (0.2mm width) for button, LEDs, and programming lines
    • Use a ground plane on the bottom layer if your tool supports it
  4. Add labels: Label the board "LED Controller v1.0", label each LED (LED1, LED2, LED3), and label the button (BTN1)

Step 4: Run Design Rules Check

  1. Configure DRC settings:
    • Minimum trace width: 0.2mm
    • Minimum clearance: 0.2mm
    • Minimum via size: 0.3mm (if using vias)
  2. Run DRC. Fix any errors (overlapping traces, too-narrow traces, etc.)
  3. Run ERC (Electrical Rule Check) on the schematic. Fix unconnected pins, missing power connections, etc.

Step 5: Export Fabrication Files

Export files in the format your lab uses:

  • For milling: Export as PNG (monochrome, high resolution, 1000 DPI) or Gerber files
  • For production: Export Gerber files (copper layers, solder mask, silkscreen) and drill files
  • Always include a drill file if your board has holes

Documentation task: Take screenshots of your schematic and board layout, annotate them to explain your design choices, and save your design files.

Recommended Software Tools

Free & Open Source
  • KiCad (Recommended): Professional-grade, completely free. Excellent for learning and production. Works on Windows, Mac, Linux.
  • Fritzing: Beginner-friendly, great for breadboard-to-PCB workflows. Limited for complex designs.
  • EasyEDA: Web-based, no installation needed. Good for low-spec computers. Requires internet.
Commercial (Free for Students/Education)
  • Autodesk Eagle: Industry standard, free for students and hobbyists. Powerful but has a learning curve.
  • Fusion 360 Electronics: Integrated with 3D CAD. Free for students and makers. Good if you already use Fusion 360.
  • Altium Designer: Professional tool, expensive. Only use if your institution provides a license.
Recommendation for UNIPOD labs: Start with KiCad. It's free, powerful, well-documented, and works on any computer. Many African labs use KiCad because it doesn't require licenses or subscriptions.

Assignments & Documentation Requirements

Individual Assignment

Design a microcontroller board that includes:

  • A microcontroller (ATtiny44/45, ATmega328P, or similar)
  • At least one input device (button, switch, sensor, etc.)
  • At least one output device (LED, buzzer, motor driver, etc.)
  • Power supply section (voltage regulator, capacitors)
  • Programming interface (ISP header, USB, or similar)
  • All necessary support components (resistors, capacitors, pull-ups, etc.)

Requirements:

  • Run ERC (Electrical Rule Check) on your schematic—fix all errors
  • Run DRC (Design Rule Check) on your board layout—configure rules for your lab's milling capabilities
  • Export fabrication files (PNG for milling or Gerber files)
  • Document your component choices: Why did you choose these parts? Are they available locally? What was the cost?

Group Assignment (Optional but Recommended)

As a UNIPOD team, create a shared component library:

  • List components that are readily available in your city/country
  • Include supplier names, approximate costs, and part numbers
  • Share EDA library files (KiCad libraries, Eagle libraries) for commonly used components
  • Document any substitutions you made (e.g., "We used LM7805 instead of AMS1117 because it's cheaper here")

Documentation Checklist

On your personal documentation page, include:

Item Description
Schematic Screenshot High-resolution image of your complete schematic with clear labels. Annotate key sections (power, microcontroller, inputs, outputs).
Board Layout Screenshot Top and bottom views of your PCB layout. Highlight important features (trace widths, clearances, component placement).
Design Files Upload your source files (.kicad_sch, .kicad_pcb, .brd, .sch, etc.) so others can learn from your design.
Fabrication Files Export and upload the files you'll send to the milling machine (PNG images or Gerber files).
Component List (BOM) Table listing all components: part name, value, package, quantity, supplier, and cost. Explain any substitutions.
Design Rationale Write 2-3 paragraphs explaining: Why did you choose this microcontroller? Why these input/output devices? How did you handle power? What design challenges did you face and how did you solve them?
DRC/ERC Reports Screenshots showing that your design passed all checks. If there were warnings, explain why they're acceptable.
Reflection What did you learn? What would you do differently next time? What resources (videos, tutorials, classmates) helped you most?
Important: Your documentation should be clear enough that another student could reproduce your board design. Include enough detail that someone reading your page understands not just what you did, but why you made each design choice.

Tips for Success

  • Start simple: Don't try to design a complex board on your first attempt. Begin with the LED controller example, then add features.
  • Use existing designs as reference: Look at open-source PCB designs (on GitHub, Hackaday, etc.) to see how others solved similar problems.
  • Test your schematic logic: Before routing, verify that your schematic makes sense. Does power flow correctly? Are all pins connected?
  • Double-check pin assignments: Microcontroller pinouts can be confusing. Always verify pin numbers match between schematic and datasheet.
  • Save frequently: EDA tools can crash. Save your work often and keep backup copies.
  • Ask for help: If you're stuck, ask your lab manager or classmates. PCB design has many "gotchas" that experienced designers know about.
  • Document as you go: Don't wait until the end to document. Take screenshots during the design process—they'll help you remember your thinking later.
